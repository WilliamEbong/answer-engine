# Spaced Retrieval Scheduling and Long-Term Retention of Procedural Skills: A Randomized Controlled Trial

**Authors:** M. Okafor, L. Brandt, S. Yuen, and P. Hallström
**Venue:** Journal of Applied Learning Science, Vol. 18, No. 4 (2025)
**DOI:** 10.5555/jals.2025.18.4.0412

## Abstract

We report a preregistered randomized controlled trial testing whether an adaptive spaced-retrieval schedule improves long-term retention of a procedural skill (sterile catheter insertion technique) compared with standard massed refresher training. Across two teaching hospitals, 412 nursing students were randomized to adaptive spacing (n = 206) or massed refreshers (n = 206) over a 16-week training window, with a blinded skills assessment at 26 weeks. The adaptive-spacing group reduced procedural error rates by 37.2% relative to the massed group and retained 84.6% of checklist steps at 26 weeks versus 61.3% in the control condition. Training time was modestly higher in the adaptive arm (median 11.4 hours vs 9.8 hours). Effects were largest for steps with high motor complexity.

## Background

Procedural skills decay quickly after initial training. Prior laboratory work on the spacing effect suggests distributing practice improves retention of declarative knowledge, but evidence for complex motor-cognitive procedures in real clinical training pipelines has been thin, and most prior studies used fixed rather than adaptive schedules.

## Methods

We enrolled 412 second-year nursing students across two sites between January and May 2025. Randomization was stratified by site and baseline skill score. The adaptive-spacing arm used a scheduling algorithm that expanded or contracted inter-session intervals based on each trainee's error profile in the preceding session; intervals ranged from 2 to 21 days. The control arm received the standard curriculum: an initial block course followed by two massed refresher sessions at weeks 8 and 16. Both arms received identical total instructional content. The primary outcome was the procedural error rate on a 43-step validated checklist, scored by two blinded assessors from video at 26 weeks (inter-rater reliability kappa = 0.87). Secondary outcomes included checklist-step retention, time-to-completion, and trainee-reported confidence.

## Results

At the 26-week blinded assessment, the adaptive-spacing group committed a mean of 3.9 errors per procedure versus 6.2 in the massed group, a relative reduction in procedural error rates of 37.2% (95% CI, 28.4% to 45.1%; p < 0.001). Checklist-step retention was 84.6% in the adaptive arm versus 61.3% in the control arm. Median cumulative training time was 11.4 hours in the adaptive arm versus 9.8 hours in the control arm, a 16.3% increase. Time-to-completion of the full procedure did not differ significantly between arms (median 14.2 vs 14.9 minutes; p = 0.21). Subgroup analysis showed the retention advantage concentrated in the 17 checklist steps rated as high motor complexity, where the adaptive arm retained 79.8% of steps versus 48.9% in controls. Trainee-reported confidence at 26 weeks was higher in the adaptive arm (mean 7.9 vs 6.8 on a 10-point scale), though confidence correlated only weakly with objective performance (r = 0.31).

## Limitations

Several limitations constrain interpretation. First, the trial evaluated a single procedural skill in a single profession; generalization to other skills or professions is not established. Second, the 26-week follow-up, while longer than most prior studies, does not speak to retention at one year or beyond. Third, the adaptive arm required scheduling software and per-trainee tracking that may be impractical in low-resource settings, and the 16.3% increase in training time carries real staffing costs. Fourth, assessors were blinded but trainees necessarily were not, and expectation effects cannot be fully excluded. Fifth, both sites were university-affiliated teaching hospitals with strong simulation infrastructure, which may inflate effects relative to typical training environments. Finally, the confidence-performance correlation of 0.31 cautions against using self-report as a proxy for competence in either arm.

## Open Questions

Whether adaptive spacing outperforms well-designed *fixed* spacing (rather than massed refreshers) remains untested here. The cost-effectiveness threshold at which the retention gain justifies the added training time is also unmodeled, as is the algorithm's sensitivity to sparse early error data.

## Terminology

- **Massed practice:** training concentrated into few, long sessions.
- **Spaced retrieval:** practice distributed over expanding intervals with active recall.
- **Checklist-step retention:** percentage of validated procedure steps performed correctly without prompting.
